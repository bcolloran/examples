PRODUCT USAGE DASHBOARD

OPEN IN NEW TAB

This is a re-creation of a dashboard that was built to give decision makers insight into long-term trends and annual variability in Firefox usage. Additionally, it provided forecasts with uncertainty estimates to guide goal-setting and to allow decision makers to track progress against projections.

IMPACT: Before this dashboard was deployed, leadership did not have a clear understanding of Firefox's usage trajectory or seasonal patterns of product usage. This would cause great consternation every June when usage numbers dropped, and excessive optimism when they recovered in October. Presenting a comprehensive overview of how these daily numbers break down into long-term trends and shorter-term cycles allowed more stable and robust long-term planning and day-to-day decision making. The value of this visualization was proved by its longevity: while most dashboards are deprecated after only 12-18 months, this one remained in active used for seven years.

DO "POWER USERS" DRIVE USAGE?

OPEN IN NEW TAB

In 2016, Mozilla launched an organization-wide push to identify our most active users and to focus product development efforts on attracting and retaining those users. There was a long-standing belief in the product organization that "heavy users" would also be "power users" (i.e. technology enthusiasts with powerful computers), and that we could target our efforts by reaching out to these people. This visualization addressed the relationship between measures of product usage and computer hardware quality.

IMPACT: By providing a clear visual overview of the relationships between usage and hardware spec, and by allowing product managers to explore these relationships and see them with their own eyes, product management was persuaded that developing user personas based around the idea of tech enthusiasts would not achieve the desired results. Because of this, product management engaged with the data science to develop a well-founded set of proxy metrics for "heavy users".

DUPLICATES IN A KEY DATA SOURCE

OPEN IN NEW TAB

In order to protect user privacy, Firefox's first major data collection effort relied on a complicated set of ID exchanges. Once deployed, it turned out that this ID handling was very error prone, and individual browser instances were submitting enormous amounts of duplicate data. This caused a critical data set to be essentially unusable for many data science purposes. The data science team presented textual reports on the issue, but many engineers involved in building the data collection system believed these analyses were incorrect and that the problem could not be as severe as described.

IMPACT: This visualization allowed engineers to see the problem in great detail with their own eyes, and this strong visual proof of the issue moved the conversation forward where textual descriptions and summary statistics had not. This convinced the engineering team that the problem was real and demanded attention, leading to a major overhaul of this essential data collection system which unblocked data science across the company.

CROSSTABS IN IMAGES

OPEN IN NEW TAB

Depicting the relationship between categorical variables can be tricky, and even more so if your story needs to show the level of confidence you can have in those relationships. This visualization of the survey responses allow viewers to quickly see the "marginal distributions" of each individual variable, as well the relationships between the variables and the statistical confidence level in the strength of those relationships.

IMPACT:These plots played an important role in survey research conducted at Mozilla around key influencers of technology adoption. Before this research project, surveys were often conducted in an ad hoc manner, and the results were often viewed skeptically. By focusing on rigor and presenting results in a manner that visually underscored the sophistication of the analysis, the research team was able to persuasively engage with the product team and successfully impact the product direction.

THE LORENZ ATTRACTOR

OPEN IN NEW TAB

Designed as a demonstration of the capabilities of the web and the Iodide IDE, this page depicts the well-known Lorenz Attractor from chaos theory. Typical 2d depictions of this complicated mathematical object fail to reveal its rich 3d structure, and static depictions fail to convey the meaning of the model's parameters and the impact of changing them. By presenting the Attractor in 3d and allowing viewers to adjust it's parameters and see the results in real time, a much deeper understanding can be achieved.

IMPACT: Despite being designed primarily as a demo for Iodide, this visualization has gone on to have a life of its own, having been visited over 30,000 times, helping to enrich many people's understanding of a mathematical topic.

IODIDE

OPEN IN NEW TAB

Iodide is a tool designed to allow data scientists to tell rich, beautiful stories using the full power of the web technology stack in a tool similar to other data science coding environments. By unlocking the enormous interaction and multimedia capabilities of the web, data scientists are able to write data-journalism-quality reports that have the visual quality you might see on fivethirtyeight.com or the New York Times Data Blog, but without needing to learn the incredibly complicated tool chains required for full scale web development.

IMPACT: Iodide achieved ~2000 monthly active users, received several media mentions, and posts about our technology reached the top spot on Hacker News twice.
